An Introduction to Pokémon Clauses

Introduction

This article aims to cover and document all rulings across RBY formats, with the aim of increasing accessibility for those interested in studying rulings, playing formats, or hosting tournaments. A big part of what makes Pokémon fun for players is the flexibility it gives in rulings, and in RBY this is no different. Rulings are put in place for various reasons, such as making Pokémon more competitive or creating new and interesting metagames for people to play. However, as more formats spring up with more rulings, it can be difficult to understand or access formats with "X Clause" listed over and over again, sometimes with obscure wording.

I will not be including simulator mods, which are used for quality of life enhancements rather than restrictions. However, should a rule employ a mod to serve its purpose on notable simulators, we will mention it in the notes. This article will also not cover individual bans, as they are not necessarily blanket rulings like what we're covering here. I've made the effort of adding some trivia for later generations as well, in an attempt to keep this interesting!

Team Restrictions

Team restrictions involve rules which dictate how a team should qualify for a battle. The most common example of this is Species Clause, which prevents multiple Pokémon of the same species from participating on a single team, though this extends to team length, event legality, and more.

Team Restrictions
Rule Short Description Detailed Notes
Team Preview Players show their teams to their opponents prior to the start of a game. This is usually enforced in games that mandate it, such as Pokémon Stadium and games after Pokémon Diamond and Pearl. In RBY, the Pokémon and Level are revealed to both players.
Team Length Limit Enforced as a part of Team Preview, players must send out Pokémon equal to (or, sometimes, less than) a given number. Enforced primarily in Nintendo formats, players would bring 3 out of the 6 Pokémon they bring to a battle. This is also part of 1v1.
Level Sum Limit Pokémon entering a battle through Team Preview cannot have their levels exceed a given number. Used in Nintendo Formats in RBY, such as Nintendo Cup 97, usually giving an effective 5 levels to distribute among Pokémon. For more information, view this article for how to apply the rule strategically.
Cleric Clause Pokémon must be healed at a Pokémon Center prior to entering a battle. This is run innately on most modern Pokémon battling simulators, such as Pokémon Showdown. The only time Cleric Clause is removed is in Anything Goes, Pure Hackmons and Balanced Hackmons.
Species Clause Pokémon of the same species cannot be used. Pokémon of the same species cannot be used, which includes form differences. For example, in modded formats where form differences could feasibly apply, you still cannot use the same species, eg. Alolan Ninetales and Kantonian Ninetales. This is in place partly due to naming conventions.
Same-Type Clause Pokémon on a given team must all share a common type. This is enforced solely in Monotype.
Event Clause Mythical Pokémon, namely Mew, are banned. Enforced primarily in Nintendo formats. Not often mentioned by name due to the Pokemon's name being specified instead. Since Mew is the only one, Event Clause is effectively enforced across all RBY formats.

Tiering and Team Restrictions

While not an explicit restriction, in Tiering-based metagames, such as Smogon and Pokemon Perfect's premier metagames, Pokemon in tiers above another are banned. For example, in UU, Pokemon in OU will be banned in addition to the tier's given banlist. The central aim and appeal of tiering is to produce metagames where any Pokemon can be used to a viable degree.

In-Battle Restrictions

While quite rare and often having little effect on a regular match, there are rules regarding what moves can be made during a battle. The most notable of these are Sleep Clause and Freeze Clause, which prevent multiple Pokémon from being, well, put to sleep or frozen by an opponent. Self-KO Clause also exists to resolve ties, dictating that the one who caused the tie loses the game.

In-Battle Restrictions
Rule Short Description Detailed Notes
Endless Battle Clause Situations that would cause an endless battle are to be avoided by both players, and sets that purposefully cause them are banned. Common RBY examples include Transform Ditto mirrors, and two Gengars using Struggle against each other, which are fixed in Stadium. There are few, if any, sets that could cause an endless battle, though in later generations Recycle + Leppa Berry is a common enabler for endless battles. Typically, this rule is enforced on platforms like Pokémon Showdown to stop the server from lagging, with battles breaching 1000 turns being declared a tie.
Desync Clause Intentionally causing a desync results in disqualification for a player. This is usually enforced on cartridge. On Pokémon Showdown, situations that cause a desync, such as cases like Psywave, are patched instead, making desync situations fail instead. This is referred to as Desync Clause Mod.
Sleep Clause Only one Pokémon can be put to sleep by the opponent. On platforms like Pokémon Showdown, as well as Stadium games, it's modded so that in the case a second Pokémon would be put to sleep, the move will miss. True to the wording, self-inflicted and pre-statused sleep does not count under Sleep Clause. However, in Pokémon Stadium games, self-inflicted sleep will count, such as through Rest. This has led to that it dubbed "Stadium Sleep Clause". Stadium Sleep Clause is still run innately in Anything Goes in Pokémon Stadium.
Freeze Clause Only one Pokémon can be frozen by the opponent. On platforms like Pokémon Showdown, as well as Stadium games, it's modded so that in the case a freeze would occur, it doesn't happen. This is run innately in Anything Goes in Pokémon Stadium. This rule is frequently contested for its lack of accuracy to mainline cartridge titles. In metagames that remove Cleric Clause, pre-statused frozen Pokemon would not count under Freeze Clause.
Self-KO Clause If both players are down to their last Pokémon, and one uses a move that would KO itself and the opponent, the user loses the battle. In RBY, this includes Self-Destruct and Explosion. This is designed to resolve ties, and is run natively in Pokémon Stadium. In RBY specifically, it displays as a tie, which is how it is normally enforced on Pokémon Showdown.
Semi-Invulnerability Clause Players who activate the Semi-Invulnerability Glitch, where Fly or Dig are interrupted and leave the player invulnerable, are to use Fly or Dig until the situation has been rectified. Not commonly used, though often proposed and sometimes enforced on cartridge. In standard play, the moves are typically just banned.

Move Restrictions

Pokémon has many similar moves, which often result in blanket rules being made to ban specific categories. The most common of these are Evasion Clause and OHKO Clause, which are often utilized to decrease the luck factor involved with winning games, thus making the game more competitive. However, there are sometimes more move bans than just these, such as the standard bans on Dig and Fly.

Move Restrictions
Rule Short Description Detailed Notes
Tradeback Clause Moves that can be obtained through trading in the Time Capsule with Pokémon Gold, Silver or Crystal are banned. This includes moves from the New York Pokémon Center events, such as Lovely Kiss Snorlax. This is enforced in all Smogon and Nintendo formats. RBY 2k20's formats remove this restriction, as our tiers are centered around the clause's removal.
Event Moves Clause Moves solely obtained via events are banned. Event Moves in RBY itself are exclusive to the Japanese games; Dragon Rage Magikarp, Fly Pikachu and Pay Day Fearow and Rapidash. Surf Pikachu can be obtained in the Stadium games, which has historically been considered to be the first Tutor Move, and as such does not fall under Event Moves Clause unless RGB 1997 is also in use. In the case of Tradebacks, this bans NYPC Moves.
RGB 1997 Clause Moves must be obtainable in Japanese Pokémon Red and Green in 1997. Name coined by RBY 2k20. This is enforced in Nintendo Cup '97 and '98 to ban Pokémon Yellow's content, as it was not released during this period, making use of them inaccurate. For more information, view this article. This bans Event Moves from later on as well. Funnily enough, this "softbans" Kinesis outside of it being used through Metronome. This restriction is usually removed in Pokémon Stadium's iterations of the formats, as it no longer flags the moves. A similar restriction is used in VGC 2017 in Pokémon Sun and Moon.
Partial Trapping Clause Partial trapping moves are banned. This includes Wrap, Bind, Fire Spin and Clamp. This has had historic use in RBY OU, though it is not enforced today. However, it does have use in metagames like 1v1 and Balanced Hackmons. It is also, effectively, used in Random Battles.
OHKO Clause Moves that inflict 65535 damage to OHKO the opposing Pokémon are banned. This includes Guillotine, Horn Drill and Fissure. In later generations, Sheer Cold is also banned under this rule. In RBYPlus, where some new OHKO moves are made, those would be banned in the case OHKO Clause is enforced.
Evasion Clause Moves that would boost Evasion are banned. This includes Double Team and Minimize. In later generations, this is sometimes used to ban abilities like Sand Veil and Moody, though it is sometimes separated under Evasion Ability Clause and Moody Clause respectively. However, the move Acupressure tends to not count.
Accuracy Clause Moves that would decrease Accuracy are banned. This includes Sand-Attack, Flash, Kinesis and Smokescreen. This is enforced solely in 1v1.
Sleep Moves Clause Moves that would put an opposing Pokémon to sleep are banned. This includes Spore, Sleep Powder, Lovely Kiss, Sing and Hypnosis. This is enforced solely in 1v1. The only case it's seen use outside of this is in Pokémon Black and White, where it's enforced in general, as switching resets the Sleep Counter there.

Conclusion

It can be difficult to understand rulings for a new player, often being given little description outside of a short line without any real clarity on the scope, so I hope this article has improved the clarity. RBY has many intricate rulings partly due to the nature of the game, so knowledge of them is key to teambuilding and success in the competitive scene.
